#The Human Element: Why You Still Own Your Work in the Age of AI

Artificial Intelligence has undeniably transformed the way we work. From drafting emails to analyzing complex datasets, AI tools are accelerating our day-to-day workflows at an unprecedented rate. But as we integrate these tools more deeply into our professional lives, a persistent question arises: If an AI helps me write or create this, whose work is it, really?

The answer is simpler and more profound than you might think: It’s still yours.

Despite the advanced capabilities of modern AI, humans retain ultimate ownership of the information and content they produce. The reason comes down to a fundamental truth about how we process knowledge: the human is the Subject Matter Expert (SME). AI is merely a sophisticated tool for gathering and assembling information; it is the human who provides the vital layers of verification, context, and ultimate responsibility.

##The Role of the Subject Matter Expert: Responsibility Equals Ownership

When an AI generates text, code, or data analysis, it is not "thinking" in the way a human does. It is predicting patterns based on vast amounts of training data. Because of this, a responsible professional must always step in to verify that the output is correct, relevant, and aligned with their specific body of knowledge and lived experience.

This requirement for human verification is not just a best practice—it is the very foundation of ownership.

Consider this: if an AI drafts a financial forecast or a legal brief that contains an error, the AI cannot be sued, fired, or held accountable. The responsibility rests entirely on the shoulders of the human professional who signed off on it. Because the human carries 100% of the risk and accountability, the human must retain 100% of the ownership. The value of the final product doesn't come from the raw data generated by the machine; it comes from the human expert who reviews, refines, and certifies that data as accurate and useful.

##We’ve Been Here Before: The Camera Precedent

If you think a machine doing the mechanical "creation" eliminates human authorship, history has a reassuring lesson for us.

In the late 19th century, the world was rocked by a disruptive new technology: the camera. Critics at the time widely dismissed photography as a mindless, purely mechanical process. They argued that because a machine captured the physical reality, a photograph could not be "authored" or copyrighted by a human.

This debate went all the way to the U.S. Supreme Court in the landmark 1884 case Burrow-Giles Lithographic Co. v. Sarony. A lithography company had copied a famous portrait of Oscar Wilde taken by photographer Napoleon Sarony, claiming that the photo was just the output of a machine and therefore free for anyone to use.

The Supreme Court disagreed. They ruled that photography was eligible for copyright because the photographer made critical, creative choices—posing the subject, arranging the draperies and lighting, and evoking the desired expression. The camera was simply a tool used to give visible form to the photographer's original mental conception.

AI operates on the exact same continuum. Just like the 19th-century camera, generative AI is a highly complex machine. But without the human "SME" to frame the prompt, guide the output, select the best results, and edit them into a finished work, the machine produces nothing of value.

##Everyday Parallels: ATMs and Search Engines

We see this same pattern play out across our daily lives with other technologies:

* The ATM and the Bank Teller: When Automated Teller Machines (ATMs) were introduced in the late 20th century, many predicted they would completely replace human tellers and take over banking. Instead, ATMs took over the mechanical, lower-value task of dispensing paper cash. This allowed human tellers to transition into higher-value financial advisors, focus on relationship banking, and solve complex customer problems. ATMs didn't strip humans of their financial agency; they freed humans to focus on tasks requiring empathy, judgment, and expertise.

* The Search Engine: When you type a query into Google, the search engine crawls the web and provides you with a curated list of information. However, no one assumes Google "wrote" your subsequent report. It is entirely up to you, the human, to click through the links, synthesize the facts, verify the accuracy of the sources, and shape it into a cohesive narrative. AI simply takes this gathering and synthesizing process a step further.

##The "Old-School" Research Assistant

We can also look outside of technology to see how intellectual ownership has historically been negotiated through delegation. Consider the dynamic between a university professor and their graduate research assistants.

A graduate student might spend weeks in the library gathering sources, running preliminary data, and compiling literature reviews. They hand that raw material over to the professor. The professor then verifies the research, contextualizes it within the broader field, makes sense of it, and weaves it into a final thesis or book.

Even though the assistant did the heavy lifting of gathering and compiling, the intellectual ownership of the final, peer-reviewed product belongs to the professor. The professor is the one putting their reputation and expertise on the line to say, "This is accurate, and this matters." AI is your ultimate, ultra-fast graduate research assistant—but you are still the professor.

##What the Law Actually Says (Sourcing the Thesis)

This isn’t just a philosophical argument; it is increasingly the legal reality.

In recent years, courts and copyright registries have had to define exactly where machine output ends and human ownership begins.

In the 2025 federal appeals court case Thaler v. Perlmutter, the D.C. Circuit Court affirmed that purely machine-generated content cannot be copyrighted, stating that human authorship remains a "bedrock requirement" of intellectual property law.

To clarify how this applies to daily work, the U.S. Copyright Office released its comprehensive report, Copyright and Artificial Intelligence, Part Two: Copyrightability. The report clarifies that while simple prompts alone do not establish ownership over an AI output, human creative selection, coordination, arrangement, and modification of AI-generated elements are fully protectable.

In other words, the law agrees with our thesis: raw AI output is just gathered information, but the moment a human expert steps in to verify, edit, arrange, and claim responsibility for it, it legally becomes human-owned work.

##A New Tool, Not a New Author

Ultimately, AI-generated content is owned by the individual who prompted it, curated it, and finalized it. AI is not a replacement for human ingenuity; it is simply a more advanced, efficient way of gathering and putting together information.

As long as we act as responsible subject matter experts—applying our critical thinking, verifying facts, and injecting our unique perspectives—we remain the true authors of our work. The tools we use to build our ideas will continue to evolve, but the human stamp of approval is what gives those ideas their true value.
